Children In Chains

"Children in Chains" is a moving and eye-opening look at child labour, revealing the brutal realities that millions of children experience throughout the world.
Child labour is the practice of employing children in jobs that rob them of their childhood, interfere with their ability to attend normal school, and are psychologically, physically, socially, or morally detrimental. The practice has changed drastically throughout the years, with substantial legislative advances aimed at protecting infants.

Our Mission

At "Children in Chains," our mission is to end child labour worldwide. We strive to create a world where every child has the opportunity to enjoy their childhood, receive a quality education, and grow up in a safe and nurturing environment. Our efforts focus on:

  • Raising Awareness: Educating the public about the harsh realities of child labour and its impact on children and society.
  • Advocacy: Working with governments and organizations to implement and enforce laws that protect children from exploitation and abuse.
  • Support and Rehabilitation: Providing support and resources to children rescued from child labour, including education, healthcare, and psychological services.
  • Community Empowerment: Engaging with communities to address the root causes of child labour, such as poverty and lack of education, and empowering families to create sustainable livelihoods.



Through our dedicated efforts, we aim to break the chains of child labour and ensure a brighter, safer future for children everywhere.

How common is child labour?

Child labour remains a persistent problem in the world today. The latest global estimates indicate that some 160 million children were engaged in child labour at the beginning of 2020 – accounting for nearly 1 in 10 children globally. Child labour is most prevalent in low-income countries, yet more than half of all child labour occurs in middle-income countries.

What are the greatest risks associated with child labour?

No matter where or how it occurs, child labour restricts children from their most fundamental rights. Many children driven into labour are cut off from schooling and health care – often at the time they need it most. In mining, they may be forced to work in deep underground shafts, to haul heavy loads of rock and to use toxic chemicals to separate minerals or precious metals from ore. On the streets, traffic and exhaust fumes threaten children’s health and safety – as does, in extreme cases, the risk of being drawn into illicit activities. These are just a few of the dangers children driven into work may face. What’s more, the isolated nature of some forms of child labour puts them at significant risk of abuse and trafficking.
